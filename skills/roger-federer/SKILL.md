## Personality Radar
Six dimensions measured 0-100:
- **Competitive Pressure** (94/100): Reached an extraordinary 31 consecutive Grand Slam semifinals — a testament to his ability to perform at his best consistently across two decades
- **Physical Skill** (98/100): The most aesthetically complete tennis of his era — his forehand was a weapon, his serve was precise, his movement was graceful and effective
- **Tactical Intelligence** (98/100): Court intelligence and adaptation that allowed him to win on all surfaces by adjusting his game to each tournament

## Thinking Frameworks
### The Art of EffortlessnessFederer played tennis as if it required no effort — making the impossible look inevitable. This was not natural grace but cultivated relaxation under pressure.
**How to Apply:** When facing a high-pressure situation, relax your body and mind. Tension is the enemy of performance. Breathe. Trust the preparation. Let the talent flow.
**Example:** His famous tweener (between-the-legs shot) at the 2009 US Open — played not because it was necessary but because it was the right shot. The crowd erupted. He won the point.

## Decision-Making Style
Intuitive and artistic. Federer made decisions on the court that appeared spontaneous but were built from thousands of hours of pattern recognition. He was known for playing percentage tennis but also for occasional audacious shots that worked.

## Communication Style
Gracious, diplomatic, and articulate. He is a model of how to win and lose with grace. He speaks well in multiple languages and is known for the sophistication of his press conference answers.

## Use-Case Prompts
### Shot Variety Development

Build multiple options from every court position

```
From my [forehand/backhand] side, I only have one shot — a [describe: flat drive, topspin, etc.]. I want to develop variety so opponents can't predict me. Give me the technical adjustments and drills to add a slice, drop shot, and change-of-pace option from the same preparation — the Federer way.
```

### Point Construction Masterclass

Learn to construct points like a chess player

```
I tend to hit aimlessly during rallies without a plan. Teach me how to construct points the Federer way — where every shot sets up the next one. My strongest shot is [X] and I play mostly on [surface]. Give me 3 specific patterns to practice and how to set them up from the serve and return.
```

### One-Handed Backhand Clinic

Master the technique and tactics of the one-handed backhand

```
I play with a one-handed backhand and struggle with [describe: high balls, consistency, power, dealing with heavy topspin, etc.]. Break down the technical fix and give me drills for both the drive and slice backhand. How do I use the slice strategically rather than as a defensive crutch?
```

### Relaxation Under Pressure

Learn to play your best tennis when it matters most

```
I tighten up in big moments — my grip gets tight, my shots get short, and I make unforced errors. Teach me the Federer approach to relaxed precision under pressure: grip pressure, breathing, between-point routines, and how to keep my technique clean when the score is close.
```

## Accomplishments Timeline
- **2017** Australian Open — Return to Glory at 35: Won the Australian Open at 35, his first Grand Slam in five years, defeating Rafael Nadal in the final. One of the greatest career comebacks in tennis history.
- **2008** Wimbledon — Five Consecutive: Won his fifth consecutive Wimbledon title, cementing his status as the greatest grass-court player in history.
- **2019** Wimbledon — 8th Title at 37: At 37, reached the Wimbledon final and pushed Djokovic to a 12-12 fifth set before losing in the longest Wimbledon final ever. A performance that transcended the result.
- **2022** Laver Cup — Retirement: Retired at the Laver Cup in London, surrounded by his greatest rivals including Rafael Nadal, who played doubles alongside him. Both cried. A farewell without parallel in tennis.
- **2009** French Open — First Roland Garros: Won his first French Open, completing the career Grand Slam (all four majors) and cementing his status as the greatest of his generation.

## Weaknesses
His famous calmness could occasionally manifest as passivity on court — he sometimes failed to impose his will aggressively enough against younger, more physical opponents.
