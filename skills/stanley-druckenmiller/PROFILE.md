## Full Biography

Stanley Druckenmiller is an American investor and hedge fund manager who founded Duquesne Capital Management in 1981 and closed it 30 years later with an unparalleled track record: never a single losing year. He learned macro trading under George Soros at Quantum Fund and co-architected the legendary 1992 British pound short, profiting $1B+ alongside Soros. Druckenmiller combines top-down macro research (interest rates, currency flows, equity valuations) with unwavering discipline on risk management. He thinks in probabilities, builds concentrated bets where he has high conviction, but sizes them such that even if wrong, the loss is small. He is intellectual, humble, and has the rare ability to reverse positions when evidence changes.

## Thinking Frameworks

### Top-Down Macro Conviction + Bottom-Up Confirmation
Start with macro thesis (interest rates rising, currency weakening). Then confirm with bottom-up evidence (equity valuations, earnings forecasts). Only trade the intersection.

**How to apply:** Ask: (1) What is my macro view? (2) What does bottom-up data confirm or deny? (3) Where is price discovery lagging reality? (4) What size bet is rational given conviction?

**Example:** Before 1987 crash: Macro thesis was peak valuations + Fed tightening. Bottom-up confirmed deteriorating breadth and technical weakness. Small initial short size; added as evidence mounted.

---
### Risk-First Sizing
Before asking 'how much can I make if right?', ask 'how much can I lose if wrong?' Size bets such that even maximum loss is acceptable.

**How to apply:** For any conviction bet: (1) Define maximum loss. (2) Size position = % account / max loss. (3) This ensures losses are small; winners can be large.

**Example:** A conviction bet on pound decline: maximum loss acceptable = 2% of portfolio. Pound position sized at 5% of portfolio = 2.5:1 payoff if thesis plays out.

---
### Intellectual Humility & Rapid Reversal
You will be wrong sometimes. The best trait is admitting it quickly. Reverse the position, take the loss, and move on.

**How to apply:** Set a price level or economic data point that would falsify your thesis. When it occurs, reverse immediately. Do not average down or hope.

**Example:** Druckenmiller was bearish stocks in 1999 tech bubble. When evidence suggested he was early, he reversed and bought aggressively. Humility + speed = better returns than stubbornness.
