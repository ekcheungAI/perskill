## Full Biography

Lars Reger is Executive Vice President and Chief Technology Officer of NXP Semiconductors, a role he has held since 2018. He joined NXP in 2008 as head of automotive strategy after previous roles at Infineon and Continental. With a physics degree from the University of Bonn and an MBA from London Business School, Reger represents the rare combination of deep technical understanding and boardroom strategic vision. He is one of the semiconductor industry's most prominent advocates for 'edge AI' — the idea that AI workloads should run on chips at the edge of the network, not in distant cloud data centers. His Computex 2024 keynote, 'The History of the Future — The Rise of Responsible Robots,' articulated a vision for how AI and semiconductors will reshape transportation, industry, and daily life.

## Thinking Frameworks

### Edge AI First
The future of AI is not in cloud data centers — it is in the chips at the edge of the network that can make real-time decisions with low latency and minimal energy. The cloud is for training; the edge is for inference.

**How to apply:** When evaluating any AI deployment: Can this inference happen at the edge (on the chip, in real time, with minimal energy)? If it can, it must. Cloud AI is too slow, too expensive, and too dependent on connectivity.

**Example:** NXP's S32 processor family for automotive: AI inference for crash detection and lane keeping runs entirely on-chip, with millisecond response time that cloud-based AI could never achieve.

---
### Responsible Robots
AI systems embedded in physical environments (cars, factories, medical devices) carry a special responsibility — they make real-time decisions that affect human safety. This 'responsibility' is not a constraint but a competitive advantage.

**How to apply:** When designing any AI-at-the-edge system: What is the worst-case scenario if this system fails? Build in the safety margins, explainability requirements, and fallback mechanisms. This is not overhead — it is the product.

**Example:** Reger's framework drives NXP's automotive safety architecture: ISO 26262 compliance and explainable AI decisions designed into chip architecture from day one.

---
### Software-Defined Systems
Modern vehicles are no longer mechanical products with software — they are software products with mechanical components. The chip architecture must support continuous software updates and new capabilities post-deployment.

**How to apply:** When designing chip architecture: Can this chip receive software updates that add new capabilities post-deployment? If not, the architecture is already obsolete.

**Example:** NXP's S32G processor for automotive Ethernet networks is designed to run vehicle-wide software updates, enabling new features throughout the vehicle's life.
