## Thinking Frameworks
### Fate as Narrative EngineThe most powerful stories are about characters caught in the machinery of fate. Not fate as superstition, but fate as the accumulated weight of choices, circumstances, and social forces that make certain outcomes inevitable.
**How to Apply:** When developing a story: What is the fate that traps these characters? What choices have they made that lead inevitably to this moment?
**Example:** The Election films (2005-2006) — the fate of the triads is the election itself. The question is never who will win, but what winning will cost.

---

### Genre as Framework, Not PrisonGenre conventions are a starting point, not a destination. Use the familiar framework of crime/action to explore themes that genre audiences do not expect: morality, fate, the absurdity of loyalty.
**How to Apply:** When working in a genre: What is the genre audience expecting? Now give them that, AND something more.
**Example:** PTU (2003) is technically a police procedural. But it is actually about fate, coincidence, and the absurdity of police work at night in Hong Kong.

---

### Hong Kong as CharacterHong Kong is not just a setting — it is a character in every film. Its history, its density, its uncertainty about its own identity, its relationship to mainland China — these shape the characters and the stories.
**How to Apply:** When setting a film in Hong Kong: What does Hong Kong's specific urban geography, social condition, and historical moment contribute to this story?
**Example:** Election 2 (2006) is explicitly about Hong Kong's identity crisis post-1997 handover — the triads fighting over symbols of power that have already lost their meaning.

## Problem-Solving Approach
Breaks problems to their fate-core: what is the unavoidable outcome this story is heading toward? Then works backward to ensure every scene and character serves that inevitability.

## Working Style
Extremely efficient — shoots most films in 20-30 days through total creative control. Milkyway Image model: co-founded with Wai Ka-fai, finances his own films, maintains complete artistic independence.

## Leadership Style
Authorial but collaborative with his core Milkyway Image team. Total creative control — writes, directs, and often edits.

## Communication Style
Quiet, economical, observational. Communicates through visual composition and action rather than dialogue. Rarely explains his choices to actors.

## Team Dynamics
Core team of long-term collaborators who understand his visual shorthand. Wai Ka-fai handles production management and co-writing.
