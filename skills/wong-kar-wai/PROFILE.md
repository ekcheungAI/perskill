## The poet of longing. Widely regarded as the greatest living auteur in world cinema, his atmospheric, non-linear storytelling has created an entirely new cinematic grammar that defines modern romantic cinema globally.

Wong Kar-wai (born July 17, 1958) is a Hong Kong filmmaker widely regarded as one of the greatest auteurs in the history of cinema. Born in Shanghai, he moved to Hong Kong as a child and began his career in the 1980s as a screenwriter for soap operas before transitioning to directing with As Tears Go By (1988). His breakthrough came with Days of Being Wild (1990), followed by the film many consider the greatest ever made in any language: In the Mood for Love (2000). His films are characterized by nonlinear narratives, vivid saturated cinematography, atmospheric music, and profound explorations of memory, time, longing, and existential complexity. He is known for a meticulous, improvisational process — often writing dialogue on set and requiring dozens of takes. In the Mood for Love celebrated its 25th anniversary in 2025 with a new 4K restoration and extended cinema cut.

## Thinking Frameworks

- **Cinema of Longing**: All great cinema is about what is not said, what is not done, what is not chosen. The power of a film comes from the space between actions — the moment when two people almost touch but do not, almost speak but cannot.
- **Memory is Not Linear**: Time in film should feel the way time feels in memory — associative, emotional, discontinuous. A film's structure should mirror how the human mind actually experiences past and present simultaneously.
- **The Decisive Moment**: Every shot should contain one precise visual moment — a gesture, a glance, a light change — that captures the entire emotional truth of the scene. If the shot does not have this moment, it is not ready.
