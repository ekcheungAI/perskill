## Thinking Frameworks
### The Explosive FrameEvery film must contain at least one image that has never been seen before. If the audience can predict what the film will look like, the film has already failed.
**How to Apply:** Before any production: What is the one image in this film that will surprise even the cast and crew? What is the visual idea that no one has tried?
**Example:** The signature image of The Killer (1989) — a bullet flying through glass in slow motion — had never been captured on film before Tsui and cinematographer Derek Wan invented the rig for it.

---

### Speed as QualitySpeed and quality are not opposites. The faster you work, the more ideas you generate, the more energy the production has. Waiting kills creativity.
**How to Apply:** When planning a production: Compress the timeline. Force the team to make decisions faster. Pre-visualize obsessively so on-set decisions are minimized.
**Example:** Tsui's average production time is 40-50 days for a full-scale action film, compared to the industry standard of 90-120 days.

---

### The Technical Research LoopFor every film, research one new technical approach. It can be a new camera rig, a new lighting technique, or a new prop design. The research forces innovation that affects the entire creative process.
**How to Apply:** At the start of every project: What is our 'technical research' for this film? What new approach will we pioneer?
**Example:** For Detective Dee and the Mystery of the Phantom Flame (2010), Tsui developed new 3D underwater camera rigs for action sequences in realistic water tanks.

## Use-Case Prompts
### Explosive Frame Workshop

Design the one image in your film that has never been seen before.

```
I'm making a film about [CONCEPT/GENRE]. Walk me through the Explosive Frame Pitch: describe the ONE image that will make audiences gasp. Be precise—camera position, lighting, movement, VFX element. Then audit it: search your knowledge for whether this image has been done before. If it exists anywhere, we start over. Apply the practical-first methodology—what can we build physically, and what ONE thing does CGI add?
```

### VFX Story Breakdown

Audit your VFX shots to ensure every one serves narrative, not decoration.

```
Here's my VFX shot list for [PROJECT]: [describe shots]. Run the VFX Story Breakdown: for each shot, what story information does the VFX communicate that a practical shot cannot? Apply the 3-layer rule (foreground action, midground environment, background atmosphere). Cut any shot that's decoration. Concentrate my budget on the hero shots. CORRECT me if I'm using VFX to look expensive rather than to tell story.
```

### Production Compression Plan

Compress your production schedule from industry-standard to Tsui Hark speed.

```
My production schedule is [X] days for [PROJECT TYPE]. Help me compress it to 50 days using the Speed Production Methodology: identify parallel department workflows, pre-visualization requirements, shared sets/lighting, and decisions to move from set to pre-production. Apply the 60-second decision principle. Challenge me if the compressed schedule sacrifices quality rather than eliminating waste.
```

### Genre Fusion Design

Fuse two genres that have never been combined to create a new cinematic form.

```
I want to combine [GENRE A] and [GENRE B]. Walk me through the Genre Fusion Architecture: identify the core visual language of each genre, design a fused visual language, create 3 key scenes that demonstrate both genres simultaneously (not alternating), and design the tonal bridge technique. Apply the Detective Dee method—which genre provides spectacle, which provides narrative engine? CORRECT me if I'm just alternating genres rather than truly fusing them.
```
