## Thinking Frameworks
### Bullet BalletViolence is not a means to an end — it is a language for expressing the most profound human emotions: loyalty, sacrifice, betrayal, and the willingness to die for an idea. The bullet ballet is an operatic form.
**How to Apply:** When staging an action sequence: What is the emotional truth this violence is expressing? Every bullet, every jump, every gun-fu movement should be driven by the character's moral stakes.
**Example:** The finale of Hard Boiled (1992) — a 40-minute unbroken action sequence in a teahouse/warehouse — is simultaneously the most technically accomplished action choreography ever filmed AND a profound meditation on the cost of loyalty.

---

### Honor Before SurvivalThe most dramatic characters are those who choose honor over survival. They know they will die. They choose to act anyway. This is the core of heroic tragedy.
**How to Apply:** When developing any action character: What would they die for? If they would not die for anything, they are not a hero yet. The question is not whether they will survive — it is whether they will stay true to their code.
**Example:** The characters in The Killer (1989) are not trying to win — they are trying to die honorably. This is what makes them heroic.

---

### Brotherhood as the Highest ValueAbove family, above love, above self-preservation — brotherhood is the highest moral value. Two men willing to die for each other is the most powerful dramatic relationship in cinema.
**How to Apply:** When developing relationships: What would each character sacrifice for the other? The answer — how much, for whom — defines the moral universe of the film.
**Example:** The Killer's core relationship — two assassins who find redemption through their willingness to die for each other. The love story is secondary; the brotherhood is the heart.

## Use-Case Prompts
### Bullet Ballet Workshop

Design an action sequence where every movement expresses a moral truth.

```
I have an action scene: [describe scene/characters]. Walk me through the Moral Choreography Map: first define what each character is willing to die for (ONE SENTENCE), then choreograph key beats that express that moral truth through movement. Apply gun-fu grammar (twin pistols = commitment, standoff = moral reckoning). CORRECT me if any beat exists purely for spectacle without moral connection.
```

### Slow-Motion Scoring

Design the speed map for an action sequence—where to use slow motion and why.

```
I have this action sequence: [describe]. Help me build the Speed Map: mark every beat as FAST, NORMAL, or SLOW. Slow motion ONLY at moral decision moments (the instant before an irreversible choice, the sacrifice reveal). Apply the 'no slow-mo rule'—maximum 2-3 slow beats per sequence. CORRECT me if I'm using slow motion for 'cool' rather than moral punctuation.
```

### Mirror Character Builder

Create a dual-protagonist structure with mirrored codes of honor.

```
Help me build two protagonists for [GENRE/SETTING]. Walk me through the Mirror Character Blueprint: define each character's code (what they die for), their method (how they apply it), what forces them into conflict, and the recognition scene where they see each other's honor. Challenge me if one character is clearly 'the good guy'—the structure requires genuine moral equivalence.
```

### Score-First Action Design

Choreograph an action sequence to music, with key moral moments landing on musical beats.

```
I want to design an action sequence scored to [MUSIC/GENRE]. Teach me the Score-First method: how to match key moral beats (gunshots, sacrifices, decisions) to musical peaks, how to use tempo changes in the music to drive tempo changes in the action, and when to use the silence technique (cutting all sound at the moral decision moment). Walk me through beat by beat.
```
